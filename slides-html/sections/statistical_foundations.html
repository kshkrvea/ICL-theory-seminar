Statistical Foundations of PFNs

When PPDs can Learn

Let \(\mathcal{S} = \{\varphi \in \Phi : \mathbb{P}_{\Phi}(\varphi) > 0\}\) be the support of the prior.
Theorem 3.1. Under regularity conditions, there exists \(\varphi^* \in \mathcal{S}\) such that for almost every \((y, x)\)

\[ \pi(y \mid x, \mathcal{D}_n) \xrightarrow[n \to \infty]{} \varphi^*(y \mid x) \quad \text{a.s.} \] and \(\varphi^*\) is the KL-optimal approximation of \(p_0\) in \(\mathcal{S}\): \(\quad \varphi^* = \arg\min_{\varphi \in \mathcal{S}} \text{KL}(\varphi \parallel p_0)\).

-> PPD trajectory does not generally lie within the prior support S for finite sample sizes: if S is non-convex — such as a union of disjoint hypothesis classes — averaging candidate models produces a predictive function that resides outside S.
-> (A1) requires only the existence within P of a unique distribution p∗ minimizing the KLD relative to p0
-> (A2) prevents the distribution П from spreading significant probability mass across an infinite number of B_j: fundamentally distinct, statistically easily distinguishable hypotheses.

PFN Approximation of the PPD

Four factors govern how well \(q_{\widehat\theta}\) approximates the family of PPDs \(\{\pi(\cdot \mid \cdot,\, D_n)\}_{n}\):

  • Model capacity \(\{q_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}\) must be expressive enough to represent \(\pi\);
  • Hypothesis space \(\Phi\) should be complex enough;
  • Monte-Carlo size: \(\widehat\theta\) \(\rightarrow\) \(\theta^*\) at rate \(\sim m^{-1/2}\) \(\Rightarrow\) a large number of steps \(m\) is needed;
  • Prior over context sizes serves as PPD's complexity regularizer.
Mechanism (for N):
- Larger n => more versatile PPD => expect approximation to model more complex functions
- Open ?: we train q by iterating over Ns => need to see large enough Ns to have complex functions
- TabPFN was trained only on N in {1, ..., 1023} yet still generalizes well to much larger n at test time -- the next section looks at why.

Bias-Variance Decomposition of PFNs

\[\begin{aligned} q_{\theta}(y \mid x, \mathcal{D}_n) - p_0(y \mid x) \;=\;& \textcolor{teal}{\underbrace{\textcolor{black}{q_{\theta}(y \mid x, \mathcal{D}_n) - \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}_n \sim p_0^n}\big[q_{\theta}(y \mid x, \mathcal{D}_n)\big]}}_{\textstyle\text{variance}}} \\[0.2em] &+\; \textcolor{blue}{\underbrace{\textcolor{black}{\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}_n \sim p_0^n}\big[q_{\theta}(y \mid x, \mathcal{D}_n)\big] - p_0(y \mid x)}}_{\textstyle\text{bias}}} \end{aligned}\]
The variance term: scatter of the prediction around its average over training sets drawn from p_0. The bias term: offset of that average from the truth. Framing (in the ? pop-up): beyond pre-training sizes the Bayesian view says nothing, so treat the pre-tuned network as an untrained predictor D_n -> q(y|x, D_n) — theta-hat is just a tuning parameter chosen by meta-learning. Empirically the error keeps decreasing with n even for sizes never seen in pre-training; the next three slides explain why: variance dies by architecture (diminishing sensitivity), bias only dies with locality.

Variance and Diminishing Sensitivity

Theorem 5.2 + Lemma 5.3 [4], intuitively: if no single sample of a large context can sway the prediction much, the variance is bounded by a vanishing ratio — and for sensitivity fading faster than \(n^{-1/2}\) it dies out almost surely. For free, at every \(n\): no pre-training on that size needed.

Read (5) as a bounded-differences / stability condition: replacing one sample moves the output by at most L n^-alpha. Theorem 5.2 is McDiarmid-style concentration: stability of order n^-alpha gives deviations of order n^(1/2-alpha); for alpha > 1/2 (Lemma 5.3) the deviation vanishes almost surely. Crucially this is a property of the ARCHITECTURE as a function of D_n, valid at any n — this is the part of the error that keeps improving on context sizes the network never saw. Foreshadow: transformers satisfy (5) by construction (softmax weights sum to 1) — that's the Transformer Networks slide. Remaining error: the bias (next slide). Viz: swap one sample of D_n, the two curves coincide as n grows; the orange band is E[q] +/- 1 std over random D_n; log-log panel shows sup|dq| ~ 1/n.

Bias and the Need for Locality

Theorem 5.4 [4], intuitively: for a generic data distribution, the bias can vanish as \(n\) grows only if the predictor is local — asymptotically, only samples near \(x_{\text{test}}\) may matter.

Intuition: if the predictor is to be asymptotically unbiased for EVERY plausible truth p, it cannot rely on far-away samples — under a different truth p-tilde those labels would be different, yet the predictor must converge to the right answer at x either way. So asymptotically only an epsilon_n-neighbourhood of x may contribute. Caveats (paper): this is only a necessary condition (the constant predictor is local but biased), and locality means the effective sample count is o(n) — a bias-variance trade-off: you cannot have (5) with alpha = 1 AND vanishing bias. Viz: the shaded band is the epsilon-window around x*; "Scramble far labels" redraws outside labels from an adversarial p-tilde — the local (window) predictor's value at x* is exactly unchanged, the global attention-like one jumps.

Statistical Foundations of PFNs

Transformer Networks

Theorem 6.2 [4], intuitively: a one-layer transformer's variance vanishes for free — deviations \(\lesssim n^{-1/2}\) (variance \(\sim 1/n\)) for any parameters \(\theta\). It is the architecture, not the training.

The attention weights of each head sum to one over the n context samples, so swapping one sample can move the attention summary by at most ~1/n — condition (5) with alpha = 1, and Theorem 5.2 turns that into the n^(-1/2) deviation bound. This holds irrespective of the tuned parameters theta-hat. The bias (Theorem 6.3) is the opposite: it depends entirely on theta, converges to a limit given by an exponentially tilted version of p_0 — attention localizes "softly" but never fully, so the bias does NOT vanish. Viz: bars are one head's softmax weights over j = 1..n; scores are fixed, growing n squashes every bar toward the dashed 1/n line; the most-attended sample (orange) can jump to a LATER sample, yet its weight still decays.

Discussion

Main insight
Transformers approximate the PPD — and keep learning beyond it.
  • with enough model capacity, a rich enough \(\Phi\) and enough Monte-Carlo steps, pre-training makes \(q_{\widehat\theta} \approx \pi\) — for context sizes from \(\mathbb{P}_N\);
  • the fourth ingredient, \(\mathbb{P}_N\), is necessarily limited (TabPFN: \(N \le 1023\)) — yet predictions keep improving on larger \(n\);
  • reason: attention forces the variance to vanish (\(\sim 1/n\)) for any \(\widehat\theta\) — generalization to unseen context sizes comes from the architecture;
  • the bias does not vanish — localization (e.g. nearest-neighbour contexts) is the remaining lever.
Wrap-up of the section's story: PPDs can learn (Thm 3.1); PFNs approximate PPDs when the four factors are right (S4); beyond pre-trained context sizes the Bayesian story ends, but the frequentist decomposition shows the variance dies by architecture (Thms 5.2/6.2), and the bias needs locality (Thm 5.4) — confirmed numerically by localized TabPFN.
